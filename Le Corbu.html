Background

Le Corbusier was born in 1887 and became one of the most influential architects of the twentieth century. He worked across architecture, urban planning, painting, and writing.

His projects aimed to combine efficiency with modern materials such as reinforced concrete. Even when people disagree with his ideas, his impact on city planning and design history is still very clear.

Key Ideas

A common theme in his work is the search for simple forms, open floor plans, and better light and ventilation in living spaces.

  • Pilotis to raise buildings from the ground
  • Roof gardens as usable open space
  • Horizontal windows for even daylight

Selected Works

Several of his buildings are part of the UNESCO World Heritage list. The table below shows a few well-known examples.

Completed in 1931 in Poissy, France, Villa Savoye represents the canonical crystallization of Le Corbusier’s “Five Points of a New Architecture”: pilotis, free plan, free façade, ribbon windows, and roof garden. The house is conceived as a purist volume elevated above the landscape, its structural grid of reinforced concrete columns liberating the interior from load-bearing walls and enabling spatial continuity. Circulation unfolds as an architectural promenade—most notably through the ramp that choreographs a gradual ascent from the shaded pilotis level to the solarium above. The building’s smooth white surfaces and horizontal glazing articulate a machine-age aesthetic, yet the roof garden reintroduces nature as an architectural component, reconciling modern construction with landscape. Villa Savoye thus operates simultaneously as manifesto and prototype, distilling the theoretical ambitions of early modernism into built form.

Completed in 1952 in Marseille, the Unité d’Habitation reinterprets collective housing as a vertical city. Conceived in the postwar context of acute housing shortages, the building integrates 337 duplex apartments within a béton brut structural frame, organized according to Le Corbusier’s Modulor proportional system. The interlocking duplex units are accessed via internal “streets” every third level, reducing circulation cores while fostering social interaction. Programmatically, the structure incorporates shops, a hotel, and a rooftop terrace equipped with recreational facilities, including a nursery and gymnasium, thereby consolidating daily life within a single megastructure. Its brise-soleil façade and deeply recessed loggias respond climatically to the Mediterranean environment, while the monumental massing anticipates later brutalist and megastructural experiments in collective form.

The Chandigarh Capitol Complex, developed during the 1950s and early 1960s as part of the new capital for the Indian states of Punjab and Haryana, constitutes a monumental civic ensemble articulating modern governance through architectural form. Comprising the High Court, Legislative Assembly, Secretariat, and the Open Hand Monument, the complex is organized along a vast esplanade framed by the Himalayan foothills. Each building deploys sculptural concrete forms—parasol roofs, monumental porticoes, and brise-soleil screens—to mediate climate and symbolize institutional authority. The Assembly’s hyperbolic roof and the High Court’s sweeping parasol canopy translate structural expression into civic iconography. As a planned governmental acropolis, the Capitol Complex synthesizes modernist abstraction with symbolic representation, positioning architecture as an instrument of national identity in a postcolonial context.
